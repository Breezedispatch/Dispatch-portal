Operating After the Tower Closes — Class G

Many Breeze stations sit in Class B, C, or D airspace only while the tower is staffed. When the tower closes, that airspace reverts to Class G. We may still continue an operation into these fields when, at the scheduled time, the airspace would have been controlled but for a temporary or emergency ATC change, weather, or a mechanical delay.

Before dispatching into Class G, confirm all four conditions are met:

  1. The airport is served by an authorized instrument approach procedure (IAP).
  2. The airport has an approved source of weather (ASOS / AWOS / D-ATIS).
  3. The PIC has a suitable means to acquire traffic advisories and field status.
  4. Facilities and services required for IFR are available and operational.

TCAS is required for all Class G tower-closed operations. Watch for field-specific exceptions — see Ogden's night restriction below.

Noise Curfews & Slot Restrictions

A handful of fields enforce hard noise-abatement curfews that dictate when we can depart or arrive. Safety and performance always take precedence over noise abatement — but outside of that, plan the operation to stay inside the curfew window and add hold fuel where slot constraints apply.

At SNA, arrivals that can't make the curfew are diverted to LAX — consult a duty manager. HPN is capped at 4 flights per 30 minutes, so carry extra hold fuel when practicable.

Terrain & Mountainous Fields

Rising terrain drives departure-procedure weight penalties, restricted night operations, and conservative planning. Verify takeoff and obstacle-departure performance early, and respect any runway- or time-of-day limits that terrain imposes.

Terrain also shapes weather — LAS sits in a mountain-ringed valley with highly variable winds, and hot/high conditions from April–October limit aircraft performance.

04{{ chapters.circle.countLabel }}

Circle-to-Land Approaches

When the instrument approach doesn't align with the landing runway, crews fly a circle-to-land maneuver. These carry special company minimums and are often daylight-only, PIC-only, and dependent on specific lighting. Whenever a circle looks probable, add a precautionary alternate or extra fuel for a go-around or two.

At SBD, roughly half the GA traffic overflown on the circle to RWY 24 has no transponder or ADS-B — visual separation is essential. Company minimums live on the 10-7 Jeppesen charts.

05{{ chapters.gdp.countLabel }}

Ground Delay Programs & Convective Weather

Summer afternoons bring thunderstorms, flow programs, ground stops, and ground delay programs (GDPs) to our busy Florida and West Coast fields. Plan hold fuel where practicable, note any EDCT in the release remarks, keep crews updated, and watch the OIS page — programs frequently roll their times out.

SFO is regularly in a GDP; when landing south the arrival rate drops sharply and program scope grows the later weather is forecast to clear. Phoenix's monsoon (Jun–Sep) adds dust storms and haboobs.

06{{ chapters.fog.countLabel }}

Fog & Low Visibility

Coastal fields are prone to early-morning and late-night advection fog and marine-layer intrusions that can drop the airport below CAT I minimums. Plan alternates and fuel accordingly, and check nearby-airport METARs for a leading indicator of what's inbound.

At LAX, nearby stations (HHR, SMO, TOA, LGB) hint at approaching visibility. SFO's geography traps cold ocean air, making it especially fog-prone in summer.

Runway & Aircraft Restrictions

Some fields limit which runways we may use, run construction closures, or apply aircraft-specific limits. Know the approved runways and any fleet difference before you plan — and remember that construction closures are frequently hard closures.

At BDL, the A220-300 is day-VFR only on RWY 15 and cannot fly the RNAV 15 — E-Jets have no such limit. VRB and ORF each have only specific approved runways.
